The Dual Narratives of Stroke Risk Factors: Lifestyle Choices vs. Predetermined Factors
Stroke, the leading cause of both disability and death on the planet, lies at the crossroads of active controversy in medicine: What causes more of it—is it lifestyle choices, or is it the hand we are dealt by genetics. A number of factors identified in stroke predictions bring out this controversy, where evidence can be found to support either of two opposing explanations. They put great emphasis on lifestyle choices, such as smoking, that individual people have great control over their stroke risk through routine habits, hence forming one of the most important determinants of health. On the other hand, the other story brings out the aspect of predestined factors, such as gender, age, and pre-existing diseases.
Dataset: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/fedesoriano/stroke-prediction-dataset

Choices and Chances: Navigating the Path to Stroke Prevention
Power of Lifestyle in Shaping Health Outcomes

This analysis clearly demonstrates that lifestyle choices, including smoking, diet, and physical activity, as reflected in BMI and average glucose levels, are critical factors in stroke risk. Our data suggests that interventions aimed at improving lifestyle choices could be key in reducing stroke incidence.

The Unseen Risks: Understanding Stroke Beyond Lifestyle
Exploring the Hidden Dimensions of Health and Risk

While lifestyle modifications are important, our findings underline the significant impact of non-modifiable factors such as age, gender, and pre-existing health conditions on stroke risk. These factors necessitate a comprehensive approach to prevention and management, including regular health screenings and personalized medical care.

Both charts utilize selective data framing as a key rhetorical technique, but to different ends: one emphasizes personal empowerment, while the other underscores the randomness of health outcomes. These methods not only draw attention to specific aspects of the data but also shape the viewer's perception, guiding them towards the intended interpretation. The persuasive efficacy of each chart stems from its ability to connect with the viewer's pre-existing beliefs and values—either the value placed on personal agency and the ability to affect one's health outcomes or the recognition of health as a complex interplay of factors often outside individual control.
